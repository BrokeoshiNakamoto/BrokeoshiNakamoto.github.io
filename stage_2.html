Eventually, in this chain of transactions, the funds reach a stage of integration. Here, they are deposited into a wallet from which they can be converted into regular currency. This conversion is facilitated by various cryptocurrency exchanges.

Based on certain heuristics, it's possible to identify a potential 'burner wallet' used for layering in transactions:

  • Low number of transactions: The wallet has a low number of both inbound and outbound transactions.
  • Zero current balance: The current balance of the wallet is zero, indicating the funds have been moved and the wallet is no longer in use.
  • Coordinated timing of transactions: The wallet might have sent funds within a similar timeframe as when it received the funds from the preceding wallet.
  • Related wallet characteristics: The wallet that either receives funds from or sends funds to the current wallet also exhibits the same characteristics as mentioned above.
  • Pattern of single-use: Wallets that appear to be used for a single transaction or series of closely timed transactions and then abandoned could be burner wallets.
  • Direct relationships with other suspicious wallets: If a wallet frequently transacts with other wallets exhibiting similar suspicious patterns, this could also be an indicator of a burner wallet.
  • Rapid movement of funds: The fast movement of funds, especially to multiple wallets in a short period, might suggest that the wallet is being used for layering.
  • Similar transaction values: If the wallet consistently sends or receives the same amount of cryptocurrency, it may be an indicator of automated or repetitive behavior associated with burner wallets.

If these conditions are met, it's likely that the wallet is a 'burner wallet.' Its sole purpose is to facilitate the layering process in these transactions. Please note, these are only indicators and not definitive proof of illicit activities. Further investigation is always necessary.

Based on certain heuristics, it's possible to identify an integration wallet:

  • Number of transactions: The wallet has a significant number of both inbound and outbound transactions.
  • Direct connections to crypto exchanges: The wallet may have direct outbound transactions to crypto exchanges.
  • Closeness to crypto exchanges: If there are no direct transactions to crypto exchanges, the wallet is within a few hops of further layered transactions until we reach a wallet that has direct transactions to crypto exchanges.
  • Volume and frequency of transactions: A wallet that routinely conducts large volume transactions or has an unusually high frequency of transactions could be a sign of integration activity.
  • Interaction with known suspicious wallets: If a wallet frequently transacts with other wallets that have been flagged as suspicious or related to illicit activities, it could potentially be involved in similar activities.
  • Age of the wallet: Newly created wallets that suddenly begin conducting large transactions can be suspicious. Criminals often use new wallets to avoid detection.
  • Wallet's connection to privacy coins or mixers: Wallets that frequently interact with privacy-focused cryptocurrencies (like Monero) or utilize mixing services to obscure the transaction trail could be considered more suspicious.
  • Inactivity periods: Wallets involved in illicit activities often have periods of inactivity between large transactions to avoid detection.
  • Consistent transaction values: If a wallet consistently sends or receives the same amount of cryptocurrency, this could indicate an attempt to avoid detection thresholds.

These heuristics can help identify potentially suspicious wallets or behaviours associated with integration. However, these are only indicators and not definitive proof of illicit activities. Further investigation is always necessary.
